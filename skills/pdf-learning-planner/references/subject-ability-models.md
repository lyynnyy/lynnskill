# Subject Ability Models

Use these as starting points. Adapt the model to the learner and the actual PDF.

## Cross-Subject Learning Model

Most subjects can be organized as six layers:

1. **Context understanding**: know what situation, text, data, or phenomenon is being studied.
2. **Information selection**: identify relevant facts, quantities, evidence, vocabulary, or constraints.
3. **Representation**: turn information into diagrams, tables, equations, notes, timelines, concept maps, or oral explanation.
4. **Relationship modeling**: explain how the parts connect.
5. **Reasoning or production**: calculate, infer, write, classify, argue, design, or solve.
6. **Explanation and validation**: justify the answer, check reasonableness, and revise.

Daily plans should visibly move the learner through these layers.

## Mathematics

Use when PDFs include arithmetic, word problems, measurement, data, geometry, money, patterns, or modeling tasks.

Stages:

1. Mathematical noticing: classify, compare, count, locate quantities.
2. Representation conversion: objects/pictures to tables, number lines, diagrams, equations.
3. One-step relationship modeling: joining, separating, comparing, change, unknown.
4. Data modeling: collect, organize, read, compare, and explain data.
5. Life-project modeling: money, time, length, shopping, schedules, measurement.
6. Multi-step modeling: choose operations, sequence steps, verify plausibility.

Checks should ask what each number represents and why an operation/model fits.

## Reading and Language Arts

Use when PDFs include phonics, vocabulary, reading passages, writing, grammar, or comprehension.

Stages:

1. Decoding or language access: sounds, words, sentence patterns, vocabulary.
2. Literal comprehension: locate who, what, where, when, and key details.
3. Structure awareness: sequence, cause/effect, compare/contrast, problem/solution.
4. Inferential comprehension: infer motives, unstated meaning, tone, or theme.
5. Evidence-based response: cite text and explain.
6. Transfer production: summarize, rewrite, discuss, or write a parallel text.

Checks should include "show where the text supports that".

## Science

Use when PDFs include observations, experiments, phenomena, classification, physical science, life science, or earth science.

Stages:

1. Phenomenon noticing: observe and describe.
2. Variable and feature selection: identify what changes, stays the same, or should be measured.
3. Representation: draw, label, table, graph, model.
4. Explanation model: cause, mechanism, system, or pattern.
5. Investigation: predict, test, compare, collect evidence.
6. Claim-evidence-reasoning: make a claim, cite evidence, explain why it supports the claim.

Checks should distinguish observation from explanation.

## History and Social Studies

Use when PDFs include timelines, maps, civic ideas, geography, biographies, primary sources, or historical narratives.

Stages:

1. Context and chronology: place, time, people, event.
2. Evidence selection: source, fact, map feature, quote, artifact.
3. Representation: timeline, map, comparison table, cause chain.
4. Causal and perspective modeling: why it happened and how people viewed it.
5. Evidence-based interpretation: claim with supporting evidence.
6. Transfer: compare with another period, place, or current situation.

Checks should ask what evidence supports the interpretation.

## Language Learning

Use when PDFs teach a second language.

Stages:

1. Input comprehension: sound/word/sentence meaning.
2. Pattern noticing: grammar, chunk, collocation, pronunciation.
3. Controlled production: repeat, fill, transform, match.
4. Communicative use: answer, ask, role-play, describe.
5. Retrieval and spacing: revisit words and patterns over days.
6. Transfer: use the pattern in a new personal context.

Checks should include comprehension and production, not only recognition.
